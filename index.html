Little penguins live in coastal colonies, travelling out to sea to feed before returning to land to rest, breed, and raise chicks. This visualisation uses tracking and breeding records to show where they move, when they are recorded, and how breeding outcomes vary across colonies.

HOW LITTLE PENGUINS LIVE

The following charts show when penguins were tracked, which individuals had the most records, and how their movements were concentrated around coastal areas.

When were penguins tracked?

This chart shows when the little penguin tracking records were collected.
The data was gathered in batches between 2008 and 2012, with some years and months sampled more heavily than others.
This helps show that the tracking dataset provides useful movement information, but its coverage is not evenly spread across time.

Which penguins were tracked the most?

Most penguins were tracked for only one day, with a small number tracked across two days.
Across the dataset there were 39 individual penguins, but tracking intensity was uneven.
The ten most-recorded penguins each had more than 900 location points, these penguins are coloured with dark blue as they are the penguins of focus.

Where did the tracked penguins travel?

Each point represents a recorded little penguin location. The tracking records show how movement is concentrated around coastal waters rather than spread evenly across Australia.

The majority of records cluster tightly around the St Kilda colony in Port Phillip Bay. A smaller group of records appears near Montague Island on the NSW south coast. Use the zoom slider and map centre dropdown to explore.

How did individual penguins move?

This map shows the at-sea foraging movements of tracked penguins from the St Kilda colony. Lines ending near the shore represent penguins returning to their nesting site on the St Kilda breakwater.

Individual movement paths differ even within the same colony. Use the checkboxes to show or hide individual penguins. Use the zoom slider to explore the tracks in detail.

RETURNING TO LAND: BREEDING & SURVIVAL

Little penguins return to coastal colonies to breed and raise chicks. This section uses records from ten South Australian colonies between 1990 and 2018 to compare breeding timing, chick survival, and colony outcomes.

Where are the ten colonies?

The ten monitored colonies are spread across Kangaroo Island and the nearby South Australian coast. Most sit on the island's sheltered north coast or its eastern tip, with Granite Island and West Island located on the Fleurieu Peninsula, and Troubridge Island further north in Gulf St Vincent.

Use the zoom slider to get a closer look at colony locations.

When does breeding happen across colonies?

Little penguins do not all breed at the same time.
This chart shows the recorded start and end of breeding seasons across the South Australian colonies, revealing that breeding timing varies between colonies

Eggs, Nestlings and Fledglings

Eggs

Eggs are the starting point of the breeding cycle. A higher egg count shows more breeding attempts, but it does not mean all chicks survive.

Nestlings

Nestlings are chicks that have hatched and are still being raised in the nest. This stage shows how many eggs progressed into young chicks.

Fledglings

Fledglings are young penguins that have grown enough to leave the nest. This is the strongest outcome measure because it shows how many chicks reached the stage where they could survive beyond the nest.

How have breeding outcomes changed across colonies?

This chart shows how fledgling numbers changed across South Australian colonies over time.
Troubridge Island stands out with a peak of 122 fledglings, while other colonies show smaller or more irregular patterns.
The checkboxes allow colonies to be compared individually, and the lower bar chart can be dragged to focus on a smaller year range.

Which colony had stronger breeding success?

This strip plot shows fledgling success for each colony.
Each dot is one year of data, and the black tick marks show each colony's average fledglings per egg.
The wide spread of points shows that breeding success varies both between colonies and from year to year.

How many eggs become fledglings?

Breeding records can be read as a progression from eggs to nestlings to fledglings.
Across most years, egg counts are higher than fledgling counts, showing that not every breeding attempt results in a chick successfully leaving the nest. Comparing the three stages helps show where numbers reduce through the breeding cycle.

Do weather conditions align with fledgling outcomes?

This chart compares fledgling counts with wind speed and rainfall.
Each point represents a colony-year, with larger circles showing years where more eggs were recorded.
The points are widely scattered, suggesting there is no clear relationship between these weather conditions and fledgling numbers in this dataset.

Which colonies experienced the most heat stress?

Little penguins are vulnerable to heat stress during the breeding season. This heatmap shows the number of days over 30°C recorded for each colony and year. Darker cells indicate more extreme heat. The white dots show fledgling rate - larger dots mean more eggs became fledglings.
